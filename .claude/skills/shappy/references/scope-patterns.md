# OT Law Scope Patterns — Reusable Scope Language

This file contains established language for scope types that appear frequently in OT Law proposals. Adapt the bracketed placeholders per matter, but keep the structural pattern intact.

---

## Corporate Incorporation

### Lead sentence
```
[Letter]. Incorporation of [Entity / Winning Groups / Company] — For [any party / the Client] that elects to incorporate, OT Law will handle the end-to-end incorporation of [up to three (3) / one (1)] [entity type] as Philippine corporation[s]. Each package is designed to give the [entity] a properly structured legal entity and to operate under Philippine law. The principal place of business [shall be / is] within [location — e.g., the National Capital Region (NCR)].
```

### Per-entity package
```
Per-[Entity] Incorporation Package:
1. Entity selection advice (regular corporation, One Person Corporation, or alternative structure as appropriate).
2. Name verification and reservation with the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC).
3. SEC registration
4. Bureau of Internal Revenue (BIR) registration
5. Barangay Clearance for the principal place of business.
6. Mayor's Permit / Business Permit from the local government unit (LGU) of the principal place of business.
```

### Exclusions block (italicize)
```
Exclusions: Government filing fees, documentary stamp taxes, notarization fees, LGU assessment fees, BIR printing of official receipts and invoices, and other third-party disbursements are excluded from the Package Fee. Registration with the Social Security System (SSS), PhilHealth, and Pag-IBIG is also excluded from the Package Fee.
```

## Trademark Registration (IPOPHL)

### Lead sentence
```
[Letter]. Trademark Registration — For [any party] that elects to protect its trademark, OT Law will file a Philippine trademark application with the Intellectual Property Office of the Philippines (IPOPHL). [The Program / This engagement] covers up to [number] ([spelled out]) marks total, with one (1) mark allocated per [party].
```

### Coverage
```
Coverage:
1. Preliminary trademark clearance search via the IPOPHL database.
2. Nice classification advice for the chosen mark (up to three (3) Nice classes per mark).
3. Preparation and filing of the trademark application with IPOPHL.
4. Monitoring of the application through publication in the IPOPHL e-Gazette and response to routine office actions, including up to two (2) responses to a Registrability Report from IPOPHL.
```

### Exclusions block (italicize)
```
Exclusions: IPOPHL government filing fees, publication fees, and per-class fees are excluded from the Package Fee. Except for the allocation of up to two (2) responses to a Registrability Report (which is included above), contested proceedings (oppositions, cancellations, appeals), additional Registrability Report responses beyond the two (2) included, and international trademark filings are not included and may be undertaken under a separate engagement. OT Law does not guarantee the approval or registration of any trademark application, which remains at the sole discretion of IPOPHL based on its examination of the mark and applicable rules.
```

## Due Diligence Engagements

### Pattern

Due diligence engagements follow a staged structure:

**Stage 1: NDA → Corporate Authorization → Exclusivity → DD Process Alignment → DD Review → DD Report**
**Stage 2 (optional): Transaction documentation and closing support**

### Lead sentence for a DD engagement
```
[Letter]. Legal Due Diligence — OT Law will conduct a legal due diligence review of [target entity / asset] on behalf of [Client / Principal]. The scope covers [property / corporate / regulatory / combination] due diligence, culminating in a written Due Diligence Report.
```

### Coverage (adapt to matter)
```
Coverage:
1. Preparation and negotiation of a Non-Disclosure Agreement (NDA) with [target / counterparty].
2. Preparation of Corporate Authorization documents for [Client] to formalize OT Law's engagement.
3. Exclusivity arrangement with [target / counterparty] as applicable.
4. Due Diligence process alignment meeting with [target / counterparty] to define document requests and scope.
5. Review of [list: corporate records / title documents / regulatory filings / material contracts / litigation / tax / labor / IP / environmental / etc.].
6. Preparation and delivery of a written Due Diligence Report covering findings, red flags, and recommendations.
```

### Payment structure note
For DD engagements, standard payment split is 40/40/20:
- 40% upon signing (non-refundable)
- 40% upon delivery of the DD Report
- 20% upon completion of Stage 2 or transaction closing

For standalone DD (no Stage 2), use 50/50 or 40/60 depending on matter complexity.

### Exclusions block (italicize)
```
Exclusions: Regulatory approvals (if any), transaction documentation beyond the DD Report, tax advisory, and contested proceedings are excluded and may be undertaken under a separate engagement. OT Law's review is limited to the documents and information made available during the course of the engagement; OT Law does not independently verify the intrinsic accuracy, truthfulness, or completeness of submitted information unless otherwise specifically agreed.
```

## Shareholder Agreements / Investment Documentation

### Lead sentence
```
[Letter]. Shareholder Agreements — OT Law will draft and negotiate up to [number] ([spelled out]) Shareholder Agreements between [Investor] and each of the [investee parties] that elect to formalize the terms of the [USD amount] investment through a Shareholder Agreement. Each agreement will be drafted to reflect the agreed terms of the investment while remaining appropriately founder-friendly for early-stage teams. OT Law will manage the drafting and walkthrough sessions with the [founders / parties] so they understand what they are signing.
```

### Mutual-agreement disclaimer
```
OT Law does not guarantee mutual agreement between the [parties] on the terms of any Shareholder Agreement; where the parties cannot reach mutual agreement, OT Law shall have no obligation to conclude a Shareholder Agreement for that [party].
```

### Note on "will" vs "may"
Use **"will"** for scope items the client is paying for. Only use "may" for genuinely optional items the recipient elects to use. Shareholder Agreement drafting is a committed deliverable when bundled into a package fee.

## Program Partnerships / Legal Education Curriculum

### Lead sentence for a curriculum / playbook offering
```
[Letter]. [Curriculum Name] — OT Law will design and deliver the [Curriculum Name] ("[Short Name]") — a [number]-module, up to [X]-hour ([spelled out]) foundational crash course in Philippine business law, tailored to [audience description]. ### Module table structure
```

Each module should have:
- Module number and title
- Session length and format (e.g., "2-hour session · Interactive lecture + practical exercise")
- Narrative description (3-5 sentences)

Based on the Founder's Legal Playbook:

2. **Running Your Business: Contracts, People, and Paperwork** (2 hrs, practical exercise)

6. **Raising Money: SAFE, Convertible Notes, and Priced Equity** (2 hrs)

## Cross-scope drafting traps

1. **Team mentions in scope vs. Appendix A II** — Appendix A II says OT Law relies on information without verification; scope subsections should be consistent (never promise independent verification unless specifically agreed).

2. **Claim windows vs. Term** — If Section III gives a claim window that extends past the Agreement Term, add the carve-out to Appendix A VII (see standard-clauses.md).

3. **"Package Fee" vs "base package"** — Use "Package Fee" consistently. "Base package" is not a defined term.

4. **VAT on reimbursements** — Reimbursements of third-party fees are pass-throughs; they are separate from the Package Fee and not subject to VAT on OT Law's end.

5. **Non-guarantee disclaimers** — Any scope item touching a third-party approval (bank, SEC, IPOPHL, LGU, BSP, FDA) must include a non-guarantee clause. This is non-negotiable.

6. **"OT Law will" vs "OT Law may"** — Audit every instance of "may" in the scope before sending. For paid deliverables, use "will." "May" should only appear for items the recipient elects to use or for discretionary items.
